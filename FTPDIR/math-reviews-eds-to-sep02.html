A unifying integral ISS framework for stability of nonlinear cascades. (English. English summary)
SIAM J. Control Optim. 40 (2002), no. 6, 1888--1904 (electronic).
93D05 (93C10)
 
References: 23 Reference Citations: 0 Review Citations: 0
The paper analyzes global asymptotic stability of nonlinear systems written in a special cascade form. The driven subsystem is assumed to be integral input-to-state stable (iISS) while the driving subsystem is supposed to be globally asymptotically stable. The main result of the paper demonstrates that if the iISS gain of the driven subsystem and the convergence rate of the driving subsystem satisfy some inequality, the cascade system is globally asymptotically stable. The result is used to develop a new observer-based backstepping design of stabilizing controllers for cascade systems with inputs. The main advantage of this new design method compared to the ISS-based procedure is that it reduces the growth of the damping term.

Reviewed by Henk Nijmeijer

Input-output-to-state stability. (English. English summary)
SIAM J. Control Optim. 39 (2001), no. 6, 1874--1928 (electronic).
93D20 (34D20 34H05)
 
References: 52 Reference Citations: 1 Review Citations: 0
The input-output-to-state stability (IOSS) property of a nonlinear control system is related to the zero-detectability property, which says that if the inputs and outputs are zero then the state must be zero as well. But IOSS is more general than zero-detectability in that it provides a norm estimate of the present state in terms of the initial condition, the norm of the past input, and the norm of the past output. This paper considers "robustly detectable" systems, which are systems for which the IOSS property holds uniformly over a class of bounded disturbances. The uniform input-output-to-state stability (UIOSS) property is introduced here as the robust extension of IOSS, in that now the usual decrescent property (also called the dissipation inequality) is replaced by one that holds uniformly over all disturbances. The main result gives a Lyapunov characterization of UIOSS: A system is UIOSS if and only if there exists a smooth Lyapunov function in which the right-hand side in the decrescent property is of the form $-\sigma_1(|x(t)|)+ \sigma_2(|y(t)|)$, where the $\sigma_{i}$ are $\kappa_{\infty}$ (or modulus) functions. Loosely speaking, then, the main result implies that a system is UIOSS if and only if there exists a smooth Lyapunov function which decreases along trajectories when the norm of the output is small relative to the norm of the state. The construction of the Lyapunov function uses the value function of an auxiliary optimal control problem, and various techniques of nonsmooth analysis are applied to smooth out the value function while still maintaining the appropiate estimates.

Reviewed by Peter R. Wolenski

Meagre functions and asymptotic behaviour of dynamical systems.
Nonlinear Anal. 44 (2001), no. 8, Ser. A: Theory Methods, 1087--1109.
34G20 (34A60 34D05 37C10 47D06)
 
References: 0 Reference Citations: 0 Review Citations: 0
The authors present a unifying framework to present various facts about the asymptotic behaviour of dynamical systems defined by abstract semiflows, finite-dimensional differential inclusions, and non-autonomous semilinear infinite-dimensional systems. The key idea is to exploit the notion of meagre functions---a measurable function $x$ from the real numbers into a metric space $X$ is said to be $C$-meagre if $C \subset X$ is nonempty and, for every closed set $K \subset X$ with $K \cap C = \emptyset$, the set $x^{-1}(K)$ has finite Lebesgue measure. Using the concept of meagreness, sufficient conditions for asymptotic stability are presented which, in particular, provide characterizations of exponential stability for linear semigroups. Furthermore, the authors are able to identify a class of functions $g$ for which convergence of an orbit $x(t) = \phi(t,\xi)$ of a nonlinear semiflow $\phi$ to the largest invariant set in $g^{-1}(0)$ is a consequence of the hypothesis $g \circ x$ belongs to $L^1$. This result directly implies that $L^p$-trajectories $(1 \le p < \infty)$ of semiflows converge to $0$ as $t \to \infty$. It is very interesting that all these results follow in an elementary manner from the concept of meagre functions.

Reviewed by Sjoerd M. Verduyn Lunel

The ISS philosophy as a unifying framework for stability-like behavior. (English. English summary)
Nonlinear control in the year 2000, Vol. 2 (Paris), 443--467,
Lecture Notes in Control and Inform. Sci., 259,
Springer, London, 2001.
93D05 (93-02)
 
References: 0 Reference Citations: 0 Review Citations: 0
Input-to-state stability (ISS) has emerged as a natural framework for a large variety of stability-related aspects of nonlinear controlled systems. This article, true to its title, focuses on the underlying "philosophy". It surveys a broad range of applications of ISS, including several very recent extensions, some of which have yet to appear as journal articles.

The survey is built about the fundamental triad of linear systems theory that consists of external stability (input to output), internal stability (input to state), and detectability (input and output to state). In each case one may choose between different measures (norms) that may quantify "small". The general guiding principle is that notions of stability be invariant under (nonlinear) coordinate changes. A key step from the finite gain condition of the linear theory ($L^\infty\rightarrow L^\infty$ point of view) to ISS is to forgo asking for specific values of the gains, and instead use topological characterizations in terms of KL-functions. Alternatively, ISS may be characterized in integral form by a dissipation inequality. Small gains and series interconnections are discussed and illustrated in examples, as are other choices for the norms.

More recent work generalizes the linear $L^2\rightarrow L^\infty$ operator gain property to the notion of "integral-input-to-state stability", and develops similar nonlinear analogues of both external stability and zero-detectability. In each case, alternative dissipative characterizations are discussed.

This article is highly recommended for anyone who wants to get a quick overview of the current state of the field, and who is interested in how the multitude of technical notions all fit together nicely in a big picture.

Reviewed by Matthias Kawski

The authors consider various notions of stability for nonlinear control systems of the form $\dot x=f(x,u)$, where the state $x$ resides in $\bold R^n$ and the input (or control) $u$ takes values in $\bold R^m$. The mapping $f$ is assumed to be locally Lipschitz and the input functions $u\colon [0,\infty)\to\bold R^m$ are assumed to be essentially bounded and measurable. The notation $t\mapsto x(t,\xi,u)$ stands for the unique maximal trajectory of the system corresponding to the initial condition $x(0)=\xi$ and input function $u$. Recall that such a maximal solution is generally defined on some maximal (with respect to inclusion) interval of the form $[0,t_{\max}(\xi,u))$. In the course of this review we also refer to the following classes of functions: ${\scr K}$ is the set of functions from $[0,\infty)$ to $[0,\infty)$ that are strictly increasing, continuous, and take the value zero at zero; ${\scr K}_{\infty}$ is the subset of unbounded functions in ${\scr K}$; $\scr L$ is the set of decreasing, continuous functions from $[0,\infty)$ to $[0,\infty)$ that converge to $0$ as the argument tends to $\infty$; $\scr{KL}$ is the set of functions from $[0,\infty)^2$ to $[0,\infty)$ that are of class ${\scr K}$ in the first argument and of class ${\scr L}$ in the second argument.

Broadly construed, the concept of input-to-state stability for such a system entails the property that bounded inputs yield bounded trajectories, but various stability notions can arise by assigning distinct precise meanings to the term "bounded". In this paper, the authors consider two such notions of stability, input-to-state stability (ISS) and integral input-to-state stability (IISS), whose definitions are as follows. The system is ISS if there exist functions $\beta\in\scr{KL}$ and $\gamma\in{\scr K}$ such that for every initial state $\xi$ and input $u$ we have $$t\in [0,t_{\max}(\xi,u))\Rightarrow |x(t,\xi,u)|\leq \beta(|\xi|,t)+\gamma(\|u\|).$$ Here $|·|$ denotes any convenient norm on a finite-dimensional Euclidean space and $\|·\|$ denotes the essential supremum norm. The system is IISS if there exist functions $\alpha,\sigma\in{\scr K}_{\infty}$ and $\beta\in\scr{KL}$ such that $$t\in [0,t_{\max}(\xi,u))\Rightarrow \alpha(|x(t,\xi,u)|)\leq \beta(|\xi|,t)+ \int_0^t \sigma(|u(s)|)\,ds$$ for every initial state $\xi$ and input $u$. Various equivalent formulations of the ISS and IISS properties have already been obtained by the second and third authors in prior work (see the paper's references) and will not be presented here.

In this paper the authors develop further equivalent characterizations of the IISS property. These characterizations not only are of interest in their own right, but also enable the authors to draw further distinctions between the ISS and IISS properties. A long-standing, central question in nonlinear control has been: "Does asymptotic controllability imply feedback stabilizability?" This article combines sophisticated tools from positional differential games and nonsmooth analysis with the evolving theory of control Lyapunov functions to authoritatively and affirmatively settle this question.

A major contribution of this paper concerns the precise meaning of "solution" for discontinuous controllers. Various notions of solution for discontinuous dynamical systems have been proposed in the existing literature, yet only this article brings together deep insights from the respective areas to develop a coherent larger framework. The notion of solution given here is an accurate model of the process used in computer control ("sample and hold"). It gives rise to a very natural definition of s-stabilizing feedback (with "s" for sampling), characterized by uniform attractiveness, overshoot boundedness, and Lyapunov stability. This article complements these natural concepts of solution and s-stability with the appropriate notion of derivative of the control Lyapunov functions: the proximal subgradient.

The theory developed here exploits a deep and intrinsic connection between invariance properties (of the control Lyapunov function) with respect to trajectories, and the characterization of these properties in terms of proximal subgradients. This connection is reflected by the agreement of the local viscosity supersolution and the upper minimax solution of the corresponding Hamilton-Jacobi equation.

The extremely well written introduction of this outstanding article lays out, in very accessible terms, the technical difficulties that for so long have stood in the way of an affirmative answer to the central problem. This is complemented with a self-contained exposition of major tools from nonsmooth analysis utilized in the construction. The stabilization strategy is based on a pointwise optimization of a smoothed version of a control Lyapunov function. Intermediate stages that achieve semiglobal practical stabilization are combined iteratively, and, with appropriate smoothing, eventually yield the desired global result.

In general, one cannot expect that discontinuous controllers exhibit the robustness properties against perturbations that make feedback control so attractive in the first place (as opposed to open-loop control). However, as the authors prove in the last few sections, the s-stabilizing feedback control constructed here is indeed insensitive to small perturbations in the system's dynamics. This highly nontrivial theorem reaffirms that these notions of solution and s-stability are indeed the right ones for control. The general robustness result is formulated in terms of differential inclusions, which makes it suitable for applications dealing with various kinds of disturbances. (It allows for actuator errors---but no claims are made about measurement errors. The latter can be dealt with nicely using robust dynamic feedback controllers.) A corollary is that asymptotic controllability is structurally stable with respect to a natural class of perturbations.

Polynomial response maps.
Lecture Notes in Control and Information Sciences, 13.
Springer-Verlag, Berlin-New York, 1979. viii+168 pp. \$9.80. ISBN 3-540-09393-1
93B15 (14D20)
 
References: 0 Reference Citations: 1 Review Citations: 1
This monograph contains an extensive collection of significant results on the problem of realizing causal shift-invariant discrete-time nonlinear input/output maps in which the present output values are sums and products of past input values (i.e., the input/output maps are polynomial). Using concepts from algebraic geometry, the author defines natural internal (state) representations having various degrees of structure, and then derives necessary and sufficient conditions for a polynomial response map to be realizable by these representations. The work is based on the author's doctoral dissertation which was completed in 1976. A brief survey of the key results follows. Let $k$ be a fixed infinite field. Given a positive integer $p$, let $\pi_j$ denote the natural projections $k^p\rightarrow k$, $j=1,2,\cdots,p$. Let $\Omega_b$ denote the space of functions $u\colonZ\rightarrow k^m$ with bounded support contained in $\{t\inZ\colon t\leq 0\}$. A map $f\colon\Omega_b\rightarrow k^p$ is called a polynomial response map if for each $j$ and $u\in\Omega_b$, $(\pi_jf)(u)=\psi_j(u(0),u(-1),\cdots)$, where $\psi_j(\xi_1,\xi_2,\cdots)$ is a formal Volterra series over $k$. The element $f(u)\in k^p$ is interpreted as the output value at time $t=1$ of a "black box" in response to the input function $u$. A $k$-system $\Sigma$ is an object $(X,P,h,x^\sharp)$, where $X$ is a $k$-space (an affine $k$-scheme) called the state space, $P\colon X\times k^m\rightarrow X$ and $h\colon X\rightarrow k^p$ are polynomial maps called the transition map and output map, respectively, and $x^\sharp$ is the initial state which satisfies $P(x^\sharp,0)=0$. The system $\Sigma$ is called a polynomial system [an almost-polynomial system] if $X$ is a variety [if there exists a variety $\hat X$ and a dominating polynomial map $g\colon\hat X\rightarrow X$]. A $k$-system $\Sigma$ is said to be canonical if $\Sigma$ is quasireachable (the closure of the reachable set is the whole state space $X$) and algebraically observable (different states are distinguishable by polynomial functions of the input/output data). A major result is that any polynomial response map $f$ is realizable by a canonical $k$-system, which is unique up to $k$-system isomorphism. It is also shown by examples that in general a polynomial response map does not have a canonical realization which is a polynomial system (i.e., the state space is a variety). Using the transcendence degree notion of dimension of a $k$-space, the author shows that $f$ is finitely realizable (the state space has finite dimension) if and only if the canonical realization is an almost-polynomial system, which is the case if and only if $f$ satisfies an algebraic difference equation. A criterion for finite realizability is given in terms of the rank of a sequence of Jacobian matrices.

The next part of the work deals with bounded polynomial response maps; that is, polynomial maps with the property that no component of the input at any fixed time is raised to a power higher than a certain bound. The realization of bounded maps is studied in terms of the notion of a state-affine system. These are polynomial systems with $X=k^n$ and $x^\sharp=0$, where the output map $h\colon k^n\rightarrow k^p$ is linear, and for each input value $u\in k^m$, $P(x,u)$ is an affine function of $x$. In other words, the defining equations of a state-affine system are given by $x(t+1)=F(u(t))x(t)+G(u(t))$, $y(t)=h(x(t))$, where $x^\sharp=0$ and $F(·)$ and $G(·)$ are polynomial matrices. A state-affine system is span canonical if it is span reachable (the reachable states span $k^n$) and observable. A major result is that any bounded and finitely realizable polynomial response map has a span-canonical state-affine realization which is unique up to isomorphism. A thorough study of realizability in the bounded case can be found in the author's paper [IEEE Trans. Circuits and Systems 26 (1979), no. 5, 342--356; MR 80b:93041]. The last part of the monograph deals with several additional topics, including a characterization of the classes of quasireachable realizations in terms of a lattice structure.

As the authors indicate, a more thorough study of such systems has been made by the first author ["On the internal realization of polynomial response maps", Ph. D. Thesis, Univ. Florida, Gainesville, Fla., 1976]. The first major result in the paper is that a system is quasi-reachable if and only if it is quasi-reachable in finite time (Proposition 5.4). Here, the authors call a system quasi-reachable just in case its state variety (set) is the Zariski closure of the reachable variety (set). The notion of quasi-reachability is perhaps more natural if one recalls that in the category of affine varieties, the categorical surjectivity of a morphism is not simply the surjectivity of the map of underlying sets---indeed, it is the condition that a map be "almost onto". Although the same phenomenon occurs with injectivity, the appropriate form of observability is handled here in a rather more complicated form. In fact, this underlies the construction, Counterexample 8.1, of two systems $\Sigma,\hat\Sigma$ with the same input-output maps, but which are not isomorphic. Indeed, in the authors' words, "The construction is based on the fact that (in the category of algebraic sets) a morphism which is bijective (one-to-one and onto) is not necessarily an isomorphism". This leads the authors to construct the (dual) notion of co-system, which they use, principally, to define algebraic observability. In passing, they note (Theorem 3.3) that systems and (affine co-systems)$^{\text{op}}$ are equivalent categories, although they prefer to define algebraic observability in the latter presentation. This is apparently what is meant by "Categorical problems like system-theoretic isomorphism are easier to study in algebraic categories like co-systems rather than in geometrical-topological categories like systems. Under the more refined (or categorical) conditions of quasi-reachability and algebraic observability, a state-space isomorphism, Theorem 8.3, is proved. It should be remarked, however, that in Sontag's thesis a realization theorem is proved as well, which necessitates enlarging the category of varieties to the category of affine schemes, which justifies, in part, the emphasis on co-systems. In addition, the authors prove, by an ingenious application of a theorem of Seidenberg's (see the references within), "Theorem 7.4. Given a polynomial system $\Sigma$ there exist two integers $t$ and $r$ (which can be computed effectively from $\Sigma$) such that the following property is true: If $L$ is a subset of $U$ of cardinality $r$, then $h^w(x_1)=h^w(x_2)$ for all $w\in L^t$ implies $h^w(x_1)=h^w(x_2)$ for all $w\in U$."

That is, the theorem gives a constructive proof (and technique) of the fact that to distinguish two states in an observable system, one only need apply finitely many input sequences.
