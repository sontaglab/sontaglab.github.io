An Abstract Approach to Dissipation 95CDC -- Keywords: dissipative systems, passivity, nonlinear stability, gap

Eduardo D. Sontag\Lambda  Department of Mathematics Rutgers University, New Brunswick, NJ 08903y

Abstract We suggest that a very natural mathematical framework for the study of dissipation --in the sense of Willems, Moylan and Hill, and others-- is that of indefinite quasimetric spaces. Several basic facts about dissipative systems are seen to be simple consequences of the properties of such spaces. Quasimetric spaces provide also one natural context for optimal control problems, and even for "gap" formulations of robustness.

This is very preliminary work, but it seems appropriate to give a conference presentation to elicit comments and suggestions, since we believe that the area has great potential for development.

1 Introduction In the pioneering work ([10]), Jan Willems introduced an abstract concept of energy dissipation. He did this in order to unify i/o and state space stability, and in particular with the purpose of understanding conceptually the meaning of Kalman-Yakubovich positive-realness (passivity) as well frequency-domain stability theorems in a general nonlinear context. His work was continued by many authors, most notably Hill and Moylan (see e.g. [3, 4]). The notions that arose from this work are now central to nonlinear control, appearing in so-called "nonlinear H1" theory, the study of "ISS" systems, and modern adaptive control approaches, among many areas. In particular, a very successful workshop at the 1994 CDC dealt with many issues related to dissipative systems.

It is perhaps remarkable that many of the proofs of the basic foundational results, in particular those dealing with the existence of maximal and minimal storage functions, repeateadly perform a certain step. This step involves showing that the triangle inequality holds for a quantity, let's call it W (x; z), which measures the minimal net change in "energy" needed in order to bring one state x to another state z. This suggests that perhaps what really matters is to have an abstract notion of "oriented distance" W (x; z) and that some of the basic facts can be formulated at that level. Once this is done, the intrinsic features of the problem are more evident, and questions of choice of particular controls become irrelevant. (This is entirely analogous to --and includes as a particular case-- the definition of a distance in a Riemannian

manifold as the infimum of the lengths of paths joining two points, or the similar construction for totally nonholonomic systems, that is, the sub-Riemannian case, in which paths are restricted to be tangent to a distribution. The concept of geodesic is implicit, but the distance itself is an object of interest.) Moreover, the "distance" W gives rise in turn to a topological space: a state z is near a state z if one can go from x to z using only a small amount of energy (or gaining energy). The standard machinery of topology then becomes in principle applicable to the study of dissipative systems.

A complication is that the standard notion of metric space is not appropriate because of the lack of symmetry: the energy needed to go from state x to state z need not be --and in fact almost never will be-- the same as needed to go from z to x. (A reverse transition may not even be possible, which one can model as requiring infinite energy, that is, W (z; x) = +1.) Thus one requires at least the nonsymmetric notion of quasi -(pseudo)metric proposed independently by Wilson and Nyemytzki in 1931 and studied by various authors since then, as well as --for studying the associated topologies-- the concept of quasi-uniformity proposed by Nachbin in 1946 (see e.g. [6, 11, 7, 1]). Actually, quasimetrics are not sufficient, because the change in energy W (x; z) may be negative as well as positive (energy gain vs. loss). Thus one needs to consider indefinite quasimetrics, which we introduce in this note.

Once that one adopts the point of view of quasimetrics, it is clear that most of optimal control would probably also benefit from such an approach. In fact, any nonreversible dynamical process with an associated cost, even problems of graph search, can be so modeled. Many mathematical structures are most naturally amenable to be treated in this way; for instance, the Hausdorff distance between two subsets A and B of a metric space is defined by first defining an oriented distance from A to B and viceversa and then taking the maximum between the two; each of these two oriented measures defines in itself a quasimetric. This has been noticed, though implicitly, in the control literature, among other places in the context of "gap" characterizations of robustness; the directed gap between two systems is defined in terms of a Hausdorff-type distance between graphs of behaviors; see e.g. [2, 8], and this quantity characterizes the largest possible change in a plant for which a given controller still stabilizes. The gap metric is then defined as a symmetrization of the directed gap, but clearly the latter is the most natural notion.

More speculatively, the abstract notion permits treating discrete and continuous variables, and even discrete and continuous time (there is no "time" explicitely appearing), and hence might provide one way of generalizing dissipation ideas to the study of "hybrid" systems.
